About the Combined Budget Stress-Test Calculator

Life is unpredictable. This calculator helps couples understand their financial vulnerability and build resilience. It tests your combined budget against job loss, emergencies, and unexpected expenses so you can plan ahead.

How to use this stress-test calculator

  • Enter your combined income, expenses, and savings.
  • Set up income loss scenarios (job loss, reduction, etc.).

  • Run the stress test to see your financial runway and recommendations.

Budget Stress-Test FAQs

Common questions about stress-testing your combined budget.

Why should couples stress-test their budget?

Stress-testing helps you prepare for financial shocks like job loss, medical emergencies, or unexpected expenses. It reveals vulnerabilities in your budget and shows how long you can survive on savings, giving you peace of mind and time to plan.

How much emergency fund should couples have?

Financial experts recommend 3-6 months of essential expenses. This calculator shows your actual runway based on your specific income, expenses, and savings, helping you determine if your emergency fund is adequate.

What's a good "survival" time for a couple?

3-6 months is considered a good cushion. Less than 3 months is concerning, while 6+ months provides strong financial security. The calculator shows your specific survival time and rates it as pass, warning, or fail.

Should both partners have income protection?

Yes, ideally. Income protection insurance can replace a portion of lost income during job loss or disability. This calculator shows the impact of job loss scenarios so you can decide if additional protection is needed.

What expenses should we cut first in a crisis?

Start with discretionary spending: dining out, subscriptions, entertainment, and luxury purchases. Then look at variable expenses like groceries and transportation. The calculator assumes fixed expenses remain but highlights where you could potentially cut.

How do unemployment benefits affect our survival time?

Unemployment benefits extend your runway significantly. Enter the monthly benefit amount and duration to see the exact impact. Even partial benefits can make a big difference in how long your savings last.

What if one partner can't work due to illness?

This scenario is similar to job loss but may include additional medical expenses. Use the custom income reduction option and add medical costs as an ongoing extra expense to get an accurate picture.

How does inflation affect our budget stress test?

Inflation reduces purchasing power over time, meaning your expenses increase while income stays the same. The calculator can simulate monthly inflation to show how it reduces your survival time.

What's the difference between fixed and variable expenses?

Fixed expenses remain constant month-to-month (rent, insurance, debt payments). Variable expenses fluctuate (groceries, entertainment, dining out). In a crisis, variable expenses are easier to cut, which this calculator accounts for.

Should we include investments in our emergency fund?

No, emergency funds should be in easily accessible accounts (savings, money market). Investments can lose value if sold during a downturn. The calculator separates emergency savings from other savings for this reason.

What if both partners lose their jobs at the same time?

This is the worst-case scenario. The calculator includes a "both lose jobs" option that shows your survival time with zero income (minus any unemployment benefits). It's a valuable stress test for your budget's resilience.

How do one-time emergency expenses affect our budget?

One-time emergencies (car repairs, medical bills) reduce your emergency fund immediately. The calculator shows the impact on your survival time and whether you need to adjust your emergency fund target.

What about ongoing extra expenses like childcare?

Ongoing expenses that aren't in your regular budget can strain your finances. The calculator lets you add these and see how they reduce your runway over time, helping you plan accordingly.

How often should couples stress-test their budget?

Run a stress test quarterly or whenever your financial situation changes — new job, raise, move, or major purchase. Regular testing helps you stay prepared and adjust your savings goals.

What if we have different risk tolerances?

This is a common dynamic. The calculator provides objective numbers to facilitate discussion. Use the results to find a compromise — maybe one partner wants a 6-month cushion while the other is comfortable with 3 months.

Should we include debt payments in the stress test?

Yes, debt payments are fixed expenses that continue regardless of income. Include them in fixed expenses to get an accurate picture of your minimum required spending during a crisis.

What if we have a mortgage vs. rent?

Both are fixed expenses included in the calculator. A mortgage might be harder to reduce than rent, but the calculator treats them the same — as essential monthly payments that continue during a crisis.

Can this calculator account for side income?

Include regular side income in your combined monthly income. For irregular side income, you might want to exclude it from the stress test to be conservative, or add it as a lower number in a separate custom scenario.

What's the minimum emergency fund for couples?

The absolute minimum is 3 months of essential expenses. However, if both partners work in unstable industries or have dependents, 6-12 months is safer. The calculator shows your specific situation so you can decide.

Is this calculator free to use?

Yes, completely free. Financial preparedness shouldn't cost money. The calculator is supported by ads to keep it accessible to everyone.
