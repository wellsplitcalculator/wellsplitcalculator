Personal spending allowance FAQs

Common questions about splitting spending money fairly.

What is a personal spending allowance?

A personal spending allowance is a set amount of money each partner gets to spend however they want, completely separate from joint bills and savings. This financial arrangement reduces money conflicts and gives each person autonomy over their own spending. It's essentially "guilt-free money" that doesn't require discussion or approval from your partner, which can significantly reduce relationship tension around finances.

Should personal allowance be equal for both partners?

In most cases, yes, especially if both partners work full-time or contribute equally to household responsibilities. Equal personal allowances promote financial fairness and prevent resentment. However, if one partner has significantly higher work-related expenses (like commuting costs or professional clothing requirements), you can adjust their share using the override feature. The calculator also lets you account for extra expenses separately so they don't reduce personal spending money.

What percentage of income should go to personal spending?

There's no universal rule, but a common approach is to allocate 50-70% to joint bills, 10-20% to savings, and the remainder (typically 10-30%) to personal spending. The right percentage depends on your cost of living, financial goals, and personal preferences. This calculator lets you experiment with different percentages and cash amounts to find what works best for your situation.

What if one partner earns significantly more than the other?

This calculator uses combined income, regardless of who earns what. Many couples choose to pool all income and split personal allowances equally to promote financial partnership and avoid power imbalances. If you prefer a proportional approach, you can manually adjust each partner's share in the second step. The key is open communication about what feels fair to both of you.

Can I use this if we keep separate bank accounts?

Absolutely. Use the calculator as a planning tool to determine how much each person should transfer to joint accounts for shared expenses and savings. The remaining amount stays in each person's personal account for their individual spending. This hybrid approach gives you the benefits of shared financial planning while maintaining some financial independence.

What about debt payments — should they be joint or personal?

This depends on the nature of the debt. Shared debts (like a mortgage or joint credit cards) should be included in joint bills. Individual debts (like personal student loans or credit cards from before the relationship) can be paid from personal allowances. The calculator is flexible — you can either include them in the joint percentage or account for them through the "extra expenses" field for each partner.

How often should we review our personal allowance amounts?

Most financial experts recommend reviewing your budget and personal allowances quarterly or whenever there's a significant change in income or expenses. Life changes like new jobs, raises, moving, or having children are all good times to revisit your spending plan. Regular check-ins help maintain financial harmony and ensure your arrangements still work for both partners.

What if we have different spending habits?

Different spending habits are one of the main reasons couples use personal allowances. By having separate "no-questions-asked" money, each partner can spend on their own priorities without judgment. The joint bills and savings are protected, while personal choices remain personal. This approach respects individual preferences while maintaining shared financial stability.

Should we include groceries in joint bills or personal spending?

Most couples include groceries in joint bills since it's a shared household expense. However, if one partner has special dietary needs or both partners eat very differently, you might allocate a portion to personal spending. The beauty of this system is that you can customize it to your situation. Just be clear about what's included in each category to avoid confusion.

What about vacations and date nights?

This is a personal choice. Some couples include vacations in the savings category, others use their personal allowances for fun activities, and many do a combination of both. Using the savings category for shared experiences like vacations can help you build memories together, while using personal allowances for individual hobbies or treats maintains independence.

Can we change our percentages later?

Absolutely. The calculator is designed to be flexible. You can experiment with different percentages or cash amounts until you find a balance that feels right. Many couples adjust their allocations over time as their income and priorities change. Regular financial check-ins are a healthy habit for any relationship.

Is it okay for one partner to have more personal spending?

That's a conversation for you and your partner. While equal allowances are recommended for fairness, every relationship is different. Some couples agree to unequal allowances due to work-related expenses, debt obligations, or other valid reasons. The key is that both partners feel the arrangement is fair and communicate openly about their needs.

What if we have irregular income?

If your income varies (freelancers, commission-based workers, etc.), base your calculations on a conservative monthly average. You can use the fixed personal allowance option to ensure consistent spending money each month, and adjust the joint and savings percentages based on your average income. Consider building a larger emergency fund during high-income months to cover leaner periods.

Should children's expenses come from joint or personal money?

Children's expenses are typically considered joint bills, especially for essential items like food, clothing, education, and healthcare. Personal allowances might be used for discretionary children's expenses like toys, entertainment, or extracurricular activities that one partner chooses to cover. The important thing is to agree on what's essential versus optional for your family.

What about gifts for each other?

This varies by couple. Some use personal allowances to buy gifts for their partner, which keeps the surprise element and ensures gifts come from "individual" money. Others have a separate gift budget in their joint bills. The calculator can support either approach — just decide what works best for your relationship.

How does this differ from the 50/30/20 rule?

The 50/30/20 rule suggests 50% for needs, 30% for wants, and 20% for savings. Our calculator is similar but tailored specifically for couples. It separates "needs" into joint bills (which couples share) and "wants" into personal allowances (which individuals control). This structure helps couples maintain shared financial responsibilities while preserving individual financial freedom.

What if we have joint savings goals?

Joint savings goals are perfect for the "Savings & goals" category. Whether you're saving for a down payment, vacation, or emergency fund, allocating a percentage of your combined income to these goals ensures you make progress together. The remaining personal allowances can be used for individual goals or completely discretionary spending.

Can this calculator handle more than two partners?

Yes! While designed for couples, the calculator can handle any number of partners. You can add multiple partners and adjust their shares individually. This makes it useful for polyamorous relationships, shared living situations, or any group that wants to split income fairly while maintaining personal spending money.

What if our expenses change month to month?

Variable expenses are common. Use the fixed personal allowance option if you want consistent personal spending each month. For joint bills and savings, you can either use percentage-based allocations (which adjust automatically with your income) or cash amounts. The calculator's flexibility lets you adapt to changing circumstances.
