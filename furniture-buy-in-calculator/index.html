About the Furniture Buy-In Calculator

Moving in together means combining furniture — and figuring out who owns what and who owes what. This calculator helps couples and roommates fairly split furniture costs, track ownership, and plan for buyouts if you ever separate.

How to use this furniture splitter

  • Enter each partner's name and choose your currency.
  • Add furniture items with prices, categories, and who owns them.
  • Choose how to split costs (equal, by income, or custom).
  • Select a buyout method (original, depreciated, or market value).
  • Calculate to see each person's share and buyout amounts.

Furniture Buy-In FAQs

Common questions about splitting furniture costs.

How should we split furniture costs when moving in together?

There are several methods: equal split (50/50) is simplest, proportional by income is fairer when incomes differ, and custom percentage gives you flexibility. The calculator helps you compare all methods.

What if one person already owns furniture?

If one person already owns furniture, they may want to keep ownership. The other person can contribute to shared furniture purchases. The calculator's ownership assignment feature tracks who owns what.

What is a furniture buyout?

A buyout is when one partner pays the other for their share of furniture when separating. This calculator shows buyout amounts based on your chosen method (original cost, depreciated value, or market value).

How does depreciation affect furniture value?

Furniture loses value over time. The calculator can apply an annual depreciation rate to show realistic current values. This is useful for fair buyouts when separating.

What's the difference between original, depreciated, and market value?

Original value is what you paid. Depreciated value applies a percentage loss each year. Market value is what you could sell it for today. Each method affects buyout amounts differently.

Should we use original or depreciated value for buyouts?

Depreciated value is often fairer since furniture loses value. However, some couples agree on original cost if they want to keep things simple. The calculator lets you compare both.

What if we buy furniture together?

Mark items as "Shared" ownership. The calculator splits costs according to your chosen method. If you separate, the buyout calculation shows who owes whom.

How do we handle gifts or inherited furniture?

Gifts and inherited furniture are typically owned by the recipient. The calculator lets you assign items to specific people or mark them as shared.

What about furniture that was a joint purchase?

Joint purchases are marked as "Shared" ownership. The calculator splits the cost based on your chosen method and calculates the buyout if you separate.

Should we include small items like decor?

You can include any items you want. The calculator's "Other" category covers decor, rugs, lamps, and smaller items. You decide what's worth tracking.

How do we split furniture if we have different budgets?

Use the income-based split method. The higher earner contributes more, making it fair based on each person's financial situation.

What if one person wants more expensive furniture?

If one partner wants premium items, they can take ownership of those items. The "Assigned to" field lets you track who chose and owns each piece.

How do we handle furniture that's used by everyone?

Furniture that's used by everyone (like a sofa or dining table) is typically shared. Mark it as "Shared" ownership and split the cost accordingly.

What's a fair annual depreciation rate for furniture?

10-20% per year is common for most furniture. Sofas and mattresses depreciate faster (15-20%), while dining tables and solid wood pieces hold value better (10-15%). The calculator's default is 15%.

Should we create a furniture agreement?

Yes, a written agreement prevents disputes. The calculator provides a summary you can use as a reference, but a formal agreement is recommended for clarity.

How do we handle furniture in a breakup?

The calculator shows buyout amounts based on your chosen method. You can use these numbers to negotiate a fair settlement — either one partner buys out the other, or you sell items and split proceeds.

What about furniture that was a gift to both of us?

Gifts to both partners are shared. Mark them as "Shared" and split the value if you separate.

How do we track who paid for what?

Use the "Assigned to" field to track who paid for each item. The calculator shows each person's total contribution.
