About the Cost-of-Living Merge Calculator

Moving in together is an exciting milestone, but it also means combining finances and figuring out your new cost of living. This calculator helps couples understand the financial impact of merging households — showing exactly how much you'll save (or spend) and helping you plan your new budget.

How to use this merge calculator

  • Enter each person's current monthly expenses in their current living situation.
  • Enter the estimated costs for your new shared home.
  • Choose how to split the new costs (equal, by income, or custom).
  • Calculate to see your combined savings, new budget, and comparison.

Cost-of-Living Merge FAQs

Common questions about combining finances when moving in together.

How much do couples typically save when moving in together?

Couples typically save 20-40% on monthly living expenses when moving in together. The savings come from sharing rent, utilities, internet, and groceries. This calculator shows your specific savings based on your current and new expenses.

What expenses are included in the merge calculation?

The calculator includes rent/mortgage, utilities, internet/phone, groceries, transportation, insurance, and other monthly expenses. You can also add one-time move-in costs for a complete financial picture.

Should we split everything 50/50 when moving in together?

Not necessarily. While 50/50 is common, many couples prefer proportional by income if there's a significant income gap. The calculator lets you compare different split methods to find what feels fair to both of you.

What about one-time moving costs?

The calculator includes a field for one-time move-in costs like furniture, moving truck, deposits, and initial setup. These costs can be significant, so it's helpful to plan for them alongside your monthly budget.

How do we handle different spending habits?

This calculator focuses on shared essential expenses. Personal discretionary spending (entertainment, shopping, hobbies) should be discussed separately. Many couples keep personal spending separate while sharing essential costs.

What if one person owns their current home?

If one person owns their home, they might have a mortgage instead of rent. The calculator works the same way — just enter the mortgage amount instead of rent. The financial analysis still shows the impact of merging.

Should we include debt payments in the merge?

This calculator focuses on living expenses. Personal debt payments (student loans, credit cards) are typically individual responsibilities and not included in shared expenses. You might want to discuss them separately as part of your overall financial plan.

What about pet expenses?

If you have pets together, include pet-related costs (food, vet, insurance) in the "other expenses" category. If only one person has a pet, you might want to exclude it from shared costs or discuss how to handle it.

How do we handle groceries?

Combined grocery expenses often decrease when moving in together because you can buy in bulk and reduce waste. The calculator shows this savings — many couples save 20-30% on groceries after merging.

What about transportation costs?

Transportation costs may change when moving. You might be closer to work, share a car, or use public transit more. The calculator helps you compare current vs. new transportation expenses.

Should we combine bank accounts right away?

Many couples keep separate accounts at first while sharing expenses. The calculator helps you determine fair contributions regardless of how you structure your accounts. Start with a shared expenses approach and consider combining accounts when you're both comfortable.

What if we have different incomes?

Proportional by income is the fairest approach when incomes differ significantly. The higher earner contributes more to shared expenses, ensuring both partners have similar disposable income percentages after bills.

How do we handle someone moving into the other's place?

If one person moves into the other's existing home, the new costs might be lower than both current costs combined. The person staying might have higher "new" costs since they're sharing their space, but overall the household saves money.

What about cost-of-living differences between cities?

Moving to a different city can significantly change your cost of living. Adjust your new cost estimates based on the destination's cost of living. The calculator works for any location — just enter accurate estimates.

How often should we review our merged budget?

Review your budget monthly for the first 3 months after moving in, then quarterly. Expenses may fluctuate as you settle in. Regular check-ins help you adjust and prevent financial stress.

What if we have different expectations about spending?

Open communication is key. Use the calculator as a starting point for discussions about money. It helps you see the numbers objectively, which can reduce emotional tension around financial decisions.

Should we include savings goals in the budget?

Yes! While not included in this calculator, it's important to discuss savings goals (emergency fund, vacation, down payment) separately. Many couples add a savings category to their shared budget.

What if one partner has children?

Child-related expenses should be considered separately. Use our Child-Free vs With-Kids Contribution Calculator for a more detailed analysis of blended family finances.

How do we handle utilities?

Utilities typically cost less per person when shared. Enter your current utility costs and your estimated new combined utility costs to see the savings. The calculator handles this comparison automatically.

Is this calculator free to use?

Yes, completely free. We believe financial planning for couples should be accessible. The calculator is supported by ads to keep it free for everyone.
