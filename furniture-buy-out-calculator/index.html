About the Furniture Buy-Out Calculator

When couples separate, dividing furniture can be one of the most stressful parts. This calculator helps you fairly value furniture, decide who keeps what, and calculate exactly how much one partner owes the other to make the division fair.

How to use this buy-out calculator

  • Enter each partner's name and choose a currency.
  • Add furniture items with original cost, age, and who takes them.
  • Choose a valuation method (original, depreciated, market, or agreed).
  • Select how to split shared items (equal, by income, or custom).
  • Calculate to see who owes whom and the final buyout amount.

Furniture Buy-Out FAQs

Common questions about furniture buy-outs during separation.

What is a furniture buy-out?

A furniture buy-out is when one partner pays the other for their share of jointly owned furniture. This allows one person to keep the items while the other receives fair compensation for their portion.

How do we value furniture for a buyout?

Common methods include original cost, depreciated value (accounting for age and wear), market value (what you could sell it for), or an agreed value. The calculator supports all four methods.

What's a fair depreciation rate for furniture?

10-20% per year is typical. Sofas and mattresses depreciate faster (15-20%), while solid wood furniture holds value better (10-15%).

How do we decide who gets what?

This is a personal decision. Some couples alternate picking items, others assign based on who needs what. The calculator shows the financial impact of any division.

What about items that are clearly one person's?

Assign them to that person. The calculator tracks each person's total value and calculates the buyout based on the difference.

How is the buyout amount calculated?

The calculator finds the difference between what each person is taking. The person taking more pays the other half the difference to equalize the division.

What if we can't agree on values?

Try the market value method or the agreed value method. You can also use multiple methods to find a compromise.

Should we include small decor items?

You decide. Track items you care about. Small items often aren't worth the effort, but the calculator can handle any number of items.

What if an item was a gift?

Gifts are typically considered the recipient's property. The calculator's assignment feature lets you mark items as belonging to specific people.

How does income affect the split?

If you use the income-based split method, higher earners would have contributed more to shared purchases, so they receive a larger share of the value.

What if we both want the same item?

You'll need to negotiate. The calculator can show the value difference if you assign it to either person, helping you make a decision.

Can we split individual items?

Yes, you can mark items as "Shared" and the calculator splits their value according to your chosen split method.

What about furniture purchased together?

These are typically shared items. Mark them as "Shared" and they'll be split according to your chosen method.

How do we handle furniture with sentimental value?

Sentimental value is personal. The calculator focuses on financial value. You may want to assign sentimental items to the person who values them most and adjust other items to balance.

What if one person has already paid more?

Use the income-based or custom percentage split method to account for unequal contributions.

Is this calculator legally binding?

No, this is a planning tool. Use it to negotiate a fair division, but consult a professional for legal agreements.

What if we have a lot of furniture?

Add as many items as you need. The calculator handles large item lists and shows summaries for clarity.

How do we split furniture if we're not married?

The same principles apply. This calculator works for any couple or roommates dividing shared furniture.

Is this calculator free to use?

Yes, completely free. We believe fair furniture division during difficult times should be accessible. The calculator is supported by ads to keep it free.
